GRAPHENE - THANK GOD FOR SCOTCH TAPE

  • Teja Kadiyala
  • Class of 2022

Recently, for the past couple of years, there has been a considerable hype in a new material called Graphene with many scientists calling it the “new wonder material”. A material that has the potential to change the world forever. So what is it? What’s so interesting about it that tech-heads and geeks are waiting for it to hit the market in the main-stream? Now, this article won’t be a nerdy and in-depth explanation of the chemical compositions of the material and such but instead be a brief explanation on what it is, why it is so important, ‘“why it hasn’t take over the world, yet”- The Verge Science’, and also the wonderful brief method of how it was first obtained.

Graphene is the thinnest material known to man-kind with a thickness of 1 atom. It is a single layer of tightly bound carbon atoms. It is obtained from Graphite with many ways to obtain it. The most simple and inefficient way to obtain graphene is, and I’m not joking about it, is taking scotch tape and sticking it to graphite and then pulling it and this was how it was first rediscovered by a Professor and his PhD student. The thin layer of carbon atoms that will be stuck on the scotch tape is graphene.

Graphene, this diverse material is almost 200 times stronger than steel and has incredible conductivity with electricity and heat.

So why is everyone excited? How would it change the world? How does it have the features that can be used in almost any industry? Like I said, this material is extremely diverse. When you think about it, Carbon is one of the most used elements in the world and even being one of the most abundant materials present, and graphene is just a mono-layer of carbon atoms which would make it have almost unlimited applications in various fields. Without the general public knowing, graphene has already made significant improvements in various fields such as energy storage, sensors, high-frequency electronics and so on.

But it still hasn’t “Changed the world”. Why? Graphene, as it is, is extremely hard to obtain and there has been no “incredibly efficient” technology for the production of it. Companies can’t keep buying scotch tapes and getting it, right? The most used way to obtain graphene is using a process called CVD (Chemical Vapour Deposition) but it is incredibly expensive and still not efficient enough.

So what happens when there is a way? Once Graphene can be mass-produced, its potential applications are advancements in batteries, computer chips, water filters, solar cells and many more. The most interesting application in my opinion is batteries which could take a huge leap in the life of a battery. Graphene batteries could last for DAYS in mobile phones (and they would be transparent) and can almost triple the mile-range in electric vehicles.
Conclusion? Isn’t it wonderful that a block of graphite and some tape can potentially change the world? Clearly, the hero of this story is Scotch Tape.
