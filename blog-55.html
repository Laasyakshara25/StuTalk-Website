Split Brain Syndrome

  • Batta Sreeya
  • Class of 2024

Everyone in this world is in a daily hunt of finding a common ground that helps nurturing relationships. Psychology is one such subject where we often find a common ground or even a mild curiosity, simply because it deals with us. The choices we make, the very existence of us is questioned which not only gives us an existential crisis but we understand who we are. These are the questions we ask ourselves, be it consciously or unconsciously.

Split Brain Syndrome, there's an instant curiosity the moment we hear it. Well, most of us know that our brain is divided into left and right brain. We even know that the left brain controls the right side of the body whereas the right brain controls the left side of the body and as common knowledge goes the left side is more associated with logic and reasoning whereas the right side with art.

Does that mean you’re two different people who have two different brains? No, most of the functions in the left brain and right brain are duplicated and it’s more of a dominance issue about which side of your body is more fluid. It’s already established that both the brains control opposite parts of your body and due to that our brain seems to have a crossover effect which requires communication between both parts of the brain.

The human mind is not a terribly logical or consistent place.

This communication is possible with the corpus callosum and it is the network of connections between the left and right brain. This is how the information from one brain is perceived by the other. Now, since our primary goal is split brain syndrome, what happens if the corpus callosum or the connection between the two brains is no more? Can we just cut the corpus callosum? A while ago, people with severe epilepsy, which can be viewed as a brainstorm, had to cut down the corpus callosum in order to decrease the storm or more commonly known as seizure.

After the incident and following the operation, patients developed acute hemispheric disconnection symptoms that lasted for days or weeks and chronic symptoms that often became permanent. Many patients with split-brain syndrome retain intact memory, social, and motor skills, even a coordinated eye movement. But what they cannot learn is to perform new tasks that require interdependent movement of each hand, such as learning to play the piano. They display unusual behavior concerning speech and object recognition as information is not being passed between the left and right brain. Finally, yes there are a lot of psychological questions and moral implications that a normal person like you and me does not have the necessary knowledge and scope to understand.
