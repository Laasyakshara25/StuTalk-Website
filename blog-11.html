ARMENIAN GENOCIDE:

A SERIES OF UNFORTUNATE EVENTS AND THE GREATEST CRIME OF THE WAR

  • R.S.AMRUTHA
  • Class of 2023

The Armenian Genocide dismantled a lot of families and cultures of Armenian Christians who were living in the Ottoman Empire. It is widely believed that the massacre first started on April 24, 1915 when 235 – 270 Armenian intellectuals and community leaders were arrested, deported, and eventually, the majority of whom were murdered. This began the tragedy which caused the deaths of more than a million people over a time period of 9 years.

Armenian families usually used to consist of extended family units like aunts, uncles, and grandparents. The Armenian religion, the traditions, and rituals in their culture played a huge part in helping Armenians around the world hold on to their identity and heritage. Only a few Armenians live in present Turkey, while around 3 million Armenians live in Armenia and more than 2 million Armenians live in Russia and the United States. Now, Armenians are scattered across the world.

During World War 1, the Armenian Genocide was carried out by the Ottoman government. A special committee called Committee of Union and Progress passed the Temporary Law of Deportation which gave the military a right to deport any person that it thought was a threat to national security. The Armenians would then be exiled and marched to the deserts of Syria. All the Armenian people including children, pregnant women, and old aged persons were forced to walk on the journey of more than 60 days in scorching heat while giving them only limited food and water and purposefully holding back supplies. Even healthy men and women could not complete the journey as the army and forces never gave enough time for the people to rest and used to periodically kill people who were not able to keep up. Many journalists and missionaries documented the journey whenever possible while trying to give them food and water.

A traveling businessman Auguste Berneau said “It was like passing through hell” in August of 1916. Over a million Armenians are believed to have died or massacred during the Genocide.

Women were raped and abused mercilessly. 8-10-year-old girls were raped in front of other deportees and subsequently shot since they could not walk as a consequence of the abuse. Many journalists could see half-naked women lie on the side of the street dead and children who were left behind by parents near bushes completely starved. The only way to get some food or water for the whole family was to bribe the soldiers, but all the wealth and possessions of the deportees were looted. The few who managed to save some money used to hide it in their bodies so that the soldiers may not find it when they tried to abuse them.

Pregnant women were not given any time to rest and were forced to walk the journey even just a day after giving birth which would eventually lead to their deaths. Many pregnant women used to cut open their wombs to remove their baby, but they would eventually die because of exhaustion and internal bleedings. In the Armenian d ense places, after the attacks, the Muslims would take all the possessions and used to eliminate men to destroy the carriers of Armenian blood. Few would rape and abuse the women and children while others married the young girls and assimilated them into Turkish households.

Even before the World War 1, during 1894-1896 when Sultan Abdul Hamid II was the ruler of the Ottoman Empire, he was nicknamed The Bloody Sultan when he created a separate military unit called Hamidiye, which consisted of armed Kurdish irregulars to “deal with the Armenians as they wished”. During the time, officials provoked the local public and instigated disruption to protest the high taxes. To counter this, the Hamidiye unit was sent to these villages and unarmed people were massacred. Many countries condemned this and forced the Sultan to change their policies, dismantle the structure of Hamidiye, and grant basic rights and equality to the Armenians. The Ottoman Empire signed a treaty but the policies in it were never enforced. To protest this, thousands of Armenians came together and asked for the implementation of the promised reforms but the police broke up the rally, and in a few days massacres of Armenian people started happening around the region. This later came to be known as the Hamidian Massacres which took the lives of over a hundred thousand Armenian people.
